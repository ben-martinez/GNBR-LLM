Plants maintain pools of pluripotent stem cells which allow them to constantly produce new tissues and organs. Stem cell homeostasis in shoot and root tips depends on negative regulation by ligand-receptor pairs of the <info type="GeneOrGeneProduct">CLE</info> peptide and <info type="GeneOrGeneProduct">leucine-rich repeat receptor-like kinase (LRR-RLK)</info> families. However, regulation of the cambium, the stem cell niche required for lateral growth of shoots and roots, is poorly characterized. Here we show that the <info type="GeneOrGeneProduct">LRR-RLK MOL1</info> is necessary for cambium homeostasis in <info type="OrganismTaxon">Arabidopsis thaliana</info>. By employing promoter reporter lines, we reveal that <info type="GeneOrGeneProduct">MOL1</info> is active in a domain that is distinct from the domain of the positively acting <info type="GeneOrGeneProduct">CLE41/PXY</info> signaling module. In particular, we show that <info type="GeneOrGeneProduct">MOL1</info> acts in an opposing manner to the <info type="GeneOrGeneProduct">CLE41/PXY</info> module and that changing the domain or level of <info type="GeneOrGeneProduct">MOL1</info> expression both result in disturbed cambium organization. Underlining discrete roles of <info type="GeneOrGeneProduct">MOL1</info> and <info type="GeneOrGeneProduct">PXY</info>, both <info type="GeneOrGeneProduct">LRR-RLKs</info> are not able to replace each other when their expression domains are interchanged. Furthermore, <info type="GeneOrGeneProduct">MOL1</info> but not <info type="GeneOrGeneProduct">PXY</info> is able to rescue <info type="GeneOrGeneProduct">CLV1</info> deficiency in the shoot apical meristem. By identifying genes mis-expressed in <info type="GeneOrGeneProduct">mol1</info> mutants, we demonstrate that <info type="GeneOrGeneProduct">MOL1</info> represses genes associated with stress-related <info type="ChemicalEntity">ethylene</info> and <info type="ChemicalEntity">jasmonic acid</info> hormone signaling pathways which have known roles in coordinating lateral growth of the <info type="OrganismTaxon">Arabidopsis</info> stem. Our findings provide evidence that common regulatory mechanisms in different plant stem cell niches are adapted to specific niche anatomies and emphasize the importance of a complex spatial organization of intercellular signaling cascades for a strictly bidirectional tissue production.
